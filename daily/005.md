## Watch

Twenty-one years later, as Voyager 1 left the solar system, Carl Sagan talked NASA into turning its camera around for one last photograph of home — a single pale pixel from four billion miles away. Then he wrote the best caption in history:

<div class="lesson-embed"><iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/GO5FwsblpT8" title="Carl Sagan — Pale Blue Dot" allowfullscreen loading="lazy"></iframe></div>

*"That's here. That's home. That's us… every saint and sinner in the history of our species lived there — on a mote of dust suspended in a sunbeam."*

## The story

A footprint that outlasts civilizations. A planet that fits in a pixel. The whole week has been about looking closer — Friday's move is looking from farther away. Both directions tell the truth; you need them in turn. Zoom in and everything matters. Zoom out and almost nothing does — except, as Sagan said, being kind to each other and preserving the dot.

Have a good weekend. Look up at least once.

## The Weekend

One film, one album, one book — things that need more than a day. No homework, no deadline. Pick one, or none.

**Film — [The Endless Summer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Endless_Summer) (1966).** Bruce Brown follows two surfers around the world chasing summer. Shot for pocket change, narrated like a letter from a friend, and quietly one of the most joyful films about curiosity ever made. It's about surfing the way *Moby-Dick* is about a whale.

**Album — [Kind of Blue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kind_of_Blue) (1959).** You heard "So What" yesterday; now take the whole forty-five minutes. Miles Davis, John Coltrane, Bill Evans — sketches, first takes, total trust. Put it on while you cook and you'll understand why it has never gone out of print.

**Book — [Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zen_and_the_Art_of_Motorcycle_Maintenance) (1974).** Robert Pirsig's road trip with his son, and the best book ever written about *quality* — the moment when caring about how something is made stops being a chore and becomes a philosophy. Rejected by 121 publishers, then sold five million copies.
