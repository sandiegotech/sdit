The Old Man, Mad About Drawing

Look

Hokusai was about seventy when he made this. He’d been drawing since he was six, and near the end of his life he signed his work Gakyō Rōjin — “the old man, mad about drawing.” His own assessment: “Until the age of seventy, nothing I drew was worthy of notice.” He believed that at ninety he would penetrate the mystery of things, and at a hundred and ten, “every dot and every stroke will be as though alive.”

He wasn’t being modest. He was telling you his theory of learning: it never finishes, and that’s the good news.

Listen

Debussy was obsessed with Japanese art — when he published La Mer, he put Hokusai’s wave on the cover of the score. Here’s the piece of his everyone knows the feeling of, even on first listen:

Clair de Lune — “moonlight.” Written by a composer looking at prints of a sea he’d never sailed.

Watch

Two minutes of Richard Feynman, gently dismantling the idea that understanding something makes it less beautiful:

“Science knowledge only adds to the excitement, the mystery and the awe of a flower. It only adds.”

The story

Here’s the thread: a seventy-year-old printmaker who thought he was just getting started, a composer who found the ocean in a woodblock print, and a physicist who saw more in the flower than his artist friend — not less.

All three are saying the same thing. Seeing is not a talent you’re born with. It’s a skill, it compounds, and it never tops out. The wave is more interesting once you know about fluid dynamics. The flower is more beautiful once you know about the cells. The moonlight is better once you know it came from a man staring at paper waves.

That’s the whole idea of this newsletter. One spark a day. Look closer.
