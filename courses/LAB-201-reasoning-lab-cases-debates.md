# LAB-201 — Reasoning Lab: Cases & Debates

Reasoning is a skill, and like all skills it only improves with practice — this lab is where you actually practice it, in public, under pressure.

## The Central Question

> How do you change someone's mind — including your own?

## What This Course Is About

This is a hands-on reasoning lab. Each session involves a case, a debate, or a structured decision exercise — and you have to prepare, perform, and reflect. You'll analyze real cases from business, policy, science, and ethics; argue positions you may not personally hold; and facilitate discussions where your job is to surface the best thinking in the room, not win the argument. The point is to build the cognitive habits that let you think clearly when the stakes are real.

## Why It Matters Now

In an environment saturated with AI-generated arguments and algorithmically curated information, the ability to reason rigorously in real time — to recognize a weak argument, steelman a strong one, and update your position based on evidence — is one of the most defensible human capacities you can develop.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Prepare and deliver concise case briefs under time pressure
- Argue assigned positions credibly, even positions you don't personally hold
- Facilitate structured discussions that produce clarity rather than noise
- Write decision logs that show your reasoning, including the paths you rejected
- Recognize cognitive biases and group dynamics as they happen in real conversations

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Cases**

- Day 1: Case anatomy — how to read a case, identify the crux, and prepare quickly
- Day 2: Ethics cases — the trolley problem is just the beginning
- Day 3: Socratic dialogue — asking better questions than giving better answers
- Day 4: Evidence critique — evaluating sources, data, and expert claims in real time
- Day 5: Case brief workshop — present, get challenged, revise

**Week 2 — Debates**

- Day 6: Structured debate format — rules, roles, and why constraints make it better
- Day 7: Steelmanning — argue the position you disagree with as well as you can
- Day 8: Negotiation simulation — interests vs. positions, BATNA, and finding the deal
- Day 9: Red-teaming — your job is to break the argument, not defend it
- Day 10: Policy design workshop — from values to workable proposal in one session

**Week 3 — Decisions**

- Day 11: Scenario planning — how to reason under uncertainty without pretending certainty
- Day 12: Stakeholder mapping — whose interests are at stake and how do they conflict?
- Day 13: Facilitation skills — running a meeting that produces a decision
- Day 14: Media literacy — applying reasoning skills to information environments
- Day 15: Final debate and reflection — your hardest case, your honest assessment

## What You'll Make

- A case brief portfolio: five written briefs from the cases analyzed in class
- A decision log documenting two structured decisions with options, trade-offs, and rationale
- A facilitation reflection: what you learned about moving groups toward clarity

## Key Practices

- Prepare every case before the session — showing up without prep is showing up to lose
- Brief, debate, decide, debrief — the loop runs every session and the debrief is not optional
- Keep a running log of when you updated your position and why
