DATA-201 — Data Science & Visualization

Data is everywhere. So is misleading data. The skill this course builds is telling the difference.

The Central Question

When a number tells you something — how do you know it’s true?

What This Course Is About

This course is applied data literacy: working with real datasets, building visualizations, evaluating statistical claims, and developing the critical instincts to distinguish genuine signal from noise. You’ll use spreadsheets and basic data tools to explore, clean, and analyze data — and then communicate what you found honestly, including what you didn’t find. The emphasis is on asking the right questions of data, not just performing calculations correctly. By the end, you’ll have completed an original data analysis project and know how to spot the five most common ways data gets used to mislead.

Why It Matters Now

Data-driven claims are everywhere in policy, journalism, product decisions, and public debate — and AI tools can now generate convincing-looking analyses on demand. The ability to evaluate whether data actually supports a claim is one of the most urgently needed critical thinking skills of the next decade. This course builds it directly.

By the End of This Course, You Will

  • Import, clean, and organize messy real-world datasets
  • Calculate and interpret descriptive statistics including mean, median, variance, and distributions
  • Build clear, honest data visualizations that communicate findings without misleading
  • Evaluate statistical claims in published research, journalism, and AI-generated analysis
  • Complete and present an original data analysis project with honest acknowledgment of limitations

The 15-Day Arc

Week 1 — Data

  • Day 1: What is data? How it’s collected, structured, and why the collection method matters
  • Day 2: Data quality — missing values, outliers, encoding errors, and what to do about them
  • Day 3: Cleaning and organizing — the unglamorous work that determines the quality of everything downstream
  • Day 4: Your first dataset — import, explore, and describe a real dataset you’ve chosen
  • Day 5: Spreadsheet fundamentals — the functions, filters, and pivot tables that unlock basic analysis

Week 2 — Analysis

  • Day 6: Descriptive statistics — mean, median, mode, variance, and when each one lies to you
  • Day 7: Distributions — what normal, skewed, and bimodal distributions look like and what they mean
  • Day 8: Correlation — the most misused concept in popular data discourse, understood properly
  • Day 9: Visualization basics — bar charts, scatter plots, histograms, and the design choices that matter
  • Day 10: Telling a story with data — the difference between a chart and an insight

Week 3 — Critical Thinking

  • Day 11: Misleading visualizations — the specific techniques used to make data lie beautifully
  • Day 12: P-values, significance, and replication — why published findings are less reliable than you think
  • Day 13: Data ethics — privacy, surveillance, algorithmic harm, and the obligations of analysis
  • Day 14: AI-generated analysis — how to evaluate claims that come from a model rather than a human
  • Day 15: Your data brief — present your original analysis publicly, defend your methods, and state your uncertainty honestly

What You’ll Make

  • A data cleaning and exploration log for a real dataset you worked with throughout the course
  • Three visualizations from your dataset, each with a written explanation of design choices and what they communicate
  • A final data brief presenting your complete analysis: question, data, methods, findings, limitations, and honest conclusions

Key Practices

  • Clean your data before you analyze it — every decision you made during cleaning is an assumption your conclusions inherit
  • Show the uncertainty — error bars, confidence intervals, and honest “I don’t know” are signs of rigor, not weakness
  • The question matters more than the tool — spend more time on what you’re asking than on how you’re calculating
