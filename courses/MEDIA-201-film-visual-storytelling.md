# MEDIA-201 — Film & Visual Storytelling

The camera is not a recording device — it's a point of view, and learning to use it is learning to think in images.

## The Central Question

> What does a camera see that your eye doesn't — and what does the edit create?

## What This Course Is About

This course is about making short films that communicate something real. You'll learn framing, light, sound, and editing — not as technical skills to master in isolation, but as expressive tools in service of a story or argument. Every two weeks you'll complete a film: planned, shot, edited, and screened. You'll give and receive honest criticism. You'll learn to work in a small crew with defined roles and shared accountability. The films don't need to be beautiful; they need to mean something.

## Why It Matters Now

Visual communication is the dominant form of public expression. The ability to produce it deliberately — to compose a shot that says what you want to say, to cut a sequence that earns an emotional response — is a genuinely powerful capability in an era where everyone has a camera and almost no one thinks carefully about how to use it.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Plan a short film with a script, shot list, and production schedule
- Capture clean audio and controlled exposure in real shooting conditions
- Edit for clarity, rhythm, and emotional impact
- Run a small production with organized roles, backups, and version control
- Give and receive specific, constructive criticism about visual storytelling choices

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Language**

- Day 1: Story basics — what images actually say and how sequences create meaning
- Day 2: Camera and composition — framing, stability, movement, and what each choice costs
- Day 3: Light — working with natural light, practical sources, and what shadows do
- Day 4: Sound — why clean dialogue is non-negotiable and how to get it
- Day 5: Scripting and shot lists — how to plan on paper so you don't improvise on location

**Week 2 — Production and Post**

- Day 6: Production 1 — crew roles, set discipline, and the first real shoot
- Day 7: Edit 1 — assembly cut, selecting your best takes, finding the structure
- Day 8: Edit 2 — rhythm, B-roll, cutaways, and what the edit reveals about the story
- Day 9: Titles, captions, and audio mix — the finishing work that separates professional from rough
- Day 10: Rights and attribution — music licensing, fair use, and the ethics of found material

**Week 3 — Depth and Portfolio**

- Day 11: Production 2 — a second, more ambitious shoot with lessons from the first applied
- Day 12: Edit 3 — incorporate feedback, re-cut, and improve
- Day 13: Color basics and export settings — making your film look like you intended it to
- Day 14: Critique and portfolio assembly — rigorous peer feedback on finished work
- Day 15: Final screening and reflection — screen your work, defend your choices, hear the response

## What You'll Make

- Three short films across the course, each with a shot list, script or treatment, and export files
- A critique record documenting the feedback you gave and received, and how it changed your work
- A portfolio package: your three films, the planning documents behind each, and a director's note

## Key Practices

- Plan on paper; shoot for the edit — every decision you make on set is a decision you don't have to make in the edit
- Record room tone and protect your audio — bad sound destroys good footage
- Watch your rough cut with fresh eyes before you refine it; the problems are easier to see when you're not attached
