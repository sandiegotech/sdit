LIFE-201 — Outdoor Leadership & Expeditionary Skills

You find out what kind of leader you are when the map doesn’t match the terrain, it’s getting dark, and your team is looking at you.

The Central Question

What do you find out about yourself when the stakes are real and there’s no safety net?

What This Course Is About

This is a field course. You’ll learn to plan and execute outdoor expeditions — route planning, navigation, campcraft, risk management, first aid — and you’ll practice leading teams in actual field conditions where decisions have real consequences. Each session follows a brief-execute-debrief cycle designed to tighten your judgment and your communication under pressure. The leadership lessons you learn here transfer directly to any context where conditions are uncertain and people are depending on you to stay clear-headed.

Why It Matters Now

The capacity to function under uncertainty — to stay calm, think clearly, and lead others when you don’t have complete information — is one of the most valuable things a person can develop. Field environments provide an honest test because they don’t reward performance; they reward competence.

By the End of This Course, You Will

  • Plan a multi-day outdoor trip including route selection, gear list, food plan, and contingency scenarios
  • Navigate using map, compass, and GPS in real terrain
  • Set up camp, manage water and food, and maintain hygiene and safety in the field
  • Lead a small team through a field exercise, including pre-brief, execution, and structured debrief
  • Apply standard risk management frameworks and execute an emergency plan if needed

The 15-Day Arc

Week 1 — Preparation

  • Day 1: Safety orientation, gear selection, and trip planning fundamentals
  • Day 2: Navigation basics — map reading, compass work, terrain association
  • Day 3: Campcraft — shelter systems, stoves, and the principles of efficient camping
  • Day 4: Water and food — purification, nutrition, and field hygiene
  • Day 5: Risk management — hazard identification, emergency protocols, and go/no-go decisions

Week 2 — Field Operations

  • Day 6: Team roles and communication — how to brief clearly and delegate with confidence
  • Day 7: Field exercise 1 — a day hike with navigation challenges and a leadership rotation
  • Day 8: Debrief and improvement plans — analyzing what happened and why
  • Day 9: Weather and terrain decisions — reading conditions and making good calls
  • Day 10: First aid basics and field scenarios — improvised care and evacuation planning

Week 3 — Leadership Under Pressure

  • Day 11: Field exercise 2 — a navigation challenge with time pressure and unknown variables
  • Day 12: Leadership rotations — every student leads a segment and is observed and debriefed
  • Day 13: Overnight prep — logistics, loadout, and the planning discipline that makes overnights successful
  • Day 14: Field exercise 3 — overnight expedition with full leadership responsibilities
  • Day 15: Final debrief and lessons learned — what you found out about yourself and what you’d do differently

What You’ll Make

  • A trip plan with route, gear list, emergency protocol, and risk assessment for a real expedition
  • A leadership reflection journal with entries from each field exercise, documenting decisions and their outcomes
  • A peer feedback summary: what your teammates observed about your leadership under pressure

Key Practices

  • Brief before every exercise, debrief after every one — the debrief is where the learning actually happens
  • Pack for conditions, not comfort — good field practice is about having what you need, not everything you might want
  • Leave no trace and respect the environment — the outdoors is borrowed, not owned
