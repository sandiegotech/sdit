# PSYCH-201 — Human Psychology

Understanding other people starts with understanding yourself — and most people are surprised by what they find.

## The Central Question

> Why do people do what they do — even when they know better?

## What This Course Is About

This course covers the core terrain of human psychology: how we perceive, remember, think, feel, decide, and relate to each other. You'll study the major frameworks — from cognitive science to social psychology to developmental theory — and apply them to real questions about learning, relationships, health, and behavior change. This is not a therapy course, but it is personal: you'll regularly apply the concepts to your own experience and reflect on what you find. The emphasis is on accurate self-knowledge and the understanding of others that follows from it.

## Why It Matters Now

AI systems are designed to model and predict human behavior. They are increasingly effective at exploiting cognitive biases, emotional vulnerabilities, and social dynamics. Understanding your own psychology — how you actually think, decide, remember, and change — is one of the most direct defenses against manipulation and one of the most powerful levers for improving your own performance and relationships.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Explain core concepts in perception, memory, cognition, and emotion with accuracy and nuance
- Identify cognitive biases and social influence mechanisms as they operate in real situations
- Apply psychological principles to questions of learning, motivation, and behavior change
- Analyze the relationship between AI system design and human psychological vulnerabilities
- Develop a more accurate model of your own psychological patterns and what shapes them

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Mind**

- Day 1: Perception and sensation — how your brain constructs reality from incomplete information
- Day 2: Memory — how it works, how it fails, and why eyewitnesses are unreliable
- Day 3: Cognition and bias — heuristics, the dual-process model, and the systematic errors in human thinking
- Day 4: Emotion and reason — the relationship between feeling and deciding, and why separating them is harder than it sounds
- Day 5: The unconscious — what happens below awareness and how it shapes behavior

**Week 2 — Behavior**

- Day 6: Motivation — intrinsic vs. extrinsic, self-determination theory, and what actually drives sustained effort
- Day 7: Habits — how they form, how they break, and the psychology behind behavior change
- Day 8: Social influence — conformity, obedience, persuasion, and the situations that override individual judgment
- Day 9: Group dynamics — in-group/out-group thinking, groupthink, and how collectives amplify individual tendencies
- Day 10: Identity — how we construct and protect our sense of self, and what happens when it's threatened

**Week 3 — Applied**

- Day 11: Psychology of learning — spacing, retrieval, interleaving, and the study strategies that actually work
- Day 12: Mental health basics — anxiety, depression, and the continuum between normal stress and clinical disorder
- Day 13: AI and human behavior — how AI systems exploit psychological vulnerabilities and what that means for design
- Day 14: Designing for humans — applying psychological principles to products, communities, and educational systems
- Day 15: Your psychological profile — a reflective synthesis of what you've learned about yourself, supported by the course concepts

## What You'll Make

- A bias journal: documented observations of cognitive biases and social influence mechanisms in your own thinking over fifteen days
- Two case analyses applying psychological frameworks to real situations — one individual, one organizational
- A personal psychological profile: a reflective synthesis connecting course concepts to your own patterns of perception, motivation, and behavior

## Key Practices

- Apply concepts to yourself before you apply them to others — the map you're building is personal, not just academic
- Notice the difference between explaining behavior and excusing it — psychology illuminates; it doesn't absolve
- Update your self-model when evidence contradicts it — the goal is accuracy, not comfort
