# MEDIA-203 — Design & Visual Communication

Design is not decoration — it is the arrangement of information in space so that the right things are understood in the right order, and this course teaches you to do that deliberately.

## The Central Question

> How do you say something clearly and beautifully without a single word?

## What This Course Is About

This is a practical design course: you'll learn layout, typography, color, and basic interaction design — and then apply them to real artifacts. You'll build a small brand identity system, design an information graphic, create a poster or one-pager, and prototype a simple interface. Every deliverable goes through a critique cycle, because design improves through specific feedback and deliberate revision, not through inspiration. No prior design experience required, but visual curiosity and attention to detail are essential.

## Why It Matters Now

The gap between good and mediocre visual communication has become visible to everyone. AI tools can now generate images and layouts, but they cannot make the judgments about hierarchy, accessibility, clarity, and intent that distinguish design from decoration. Those judgments are what this course builds.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Apply principles of hierarchy, contrast, alignment, and proximity to real design problems
- Build a typographic system and color palette that serves the content they're applied to
- Create information graphics that communicate data accurately and without misleading
- Prototype low-fidelity interfaces with clear flows and user-appropriate feedback
- Give and receive rigorous design critique, and revise your work based on it

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Principles**

- Day 1: Hierarchy, contrast, alignment, and proximity — the four principles that explain most good design
- Day 2: Typography — families, scales, pairing, and the white space that makes type readable
- Day 3: Color — palette construction, meaning, and accessibility from the start
- Day 4: Layout — grids, rhythm, and how spatial organization creates comprehension
- Day 5: Imagery and iconography — how to choose and use visual elements that serve the message

**Week 2 — Systems**

- Day 6: Brand basics — how a consistent visual language creates recognition and trust
- Day 7: Information design — clarity and honesty as design values, not just aesthetics
- Day 8: Prototyping — wireframes, flows, and the tools that let you test before you build
- Day 9: Interaction patterns — states, feedback, and how design communicates behavior
- Day 10: Accessibility checks — designing for the range of how people actually perceive and use things

**Week 3 — Critique and Portfolio**

- Day 11: Critique 1 — poster or one-pager, evaluated for clarity and hierarchy
- Day 12: Critique 2 — information graphic, evaluated for accuracy and visual honesty
- Day 13: Critique 3 — interface prototype, evaluated for usability and flow
- Day 14: Polish and packaging — preparing your work for delivery and presentation
- Day 15: Portfolio review and reflection — present your work, explain your decisions, update your craft notes

## What You'll Make

- A brand kit including logo, typographic scale, color system, and usage examples
- An information graphic and a poster or one-pager, each with a critique record
- A low-fidelity interactive prototype with documented user flows

## Key Practices

- Start low-fidelity and stay there until the structure is right — adding detail before solving hierarchy is a trap
- Design for the least-advantaged user first — accessibility is not a feature, it's the foundation
- Write alt text and label your data — design that communicates only visually is incomplete design
