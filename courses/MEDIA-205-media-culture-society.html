MEDIA-205 — Media, Culture & Society

The media environment you grew up in was not neutral — it was designed, and understanding how it was designed is the beginning of not being controlled by it.

The Central Question

Who controls which stories get told — and what does that do to everyone else?

What This Course Is About

This course examines how media technologies and platforms shape attention, identity, discourse, and power — and what it means to participate in that environment with awareness. You’ll analyze media systems through frameworks from communication theory, political economy, and sociology, and apply them to real cases: the news ecosystem, creator economies, algorithmic content curation, misinformation, and digital mobilization. You’ll learn to write about media with rigor and specificity, because the quality of public conversation about these systems matters enormously.

Why It Matters Now

The platforms mediating public life were built with specific incentive structures that systematically reward engagement over accuracy, outrage over nuance, and scale over trust. Understanding those structures doesn’t make you immune to them — but it makes you a more deliberate participant, and a better designer of the alternatives.

By the End of This Course, You Will

  • Apply key media theories — agenda-setting, framing, network effects, platform governance — to contemporary cases
  • Map the incentive structures and governance arrangements of major media platforms
  • Evaluate claims, sources, and evidence with media-specific critical frameworks
  • Write clear, decision-ready media analysis for practitioners and policymakers
  • Present findings to non-expert audiences without losing analytical rigor

The 15-Day Arc

Week 1 — Systems

  • Day 1: What is media? Forms, functions, and the history that explains the present
  • Day 2: Attention and algorithms — how feeds work and whose interests they serve
  • Day 3: Networks and virality — why some things spread and what happens when they do
  • Day 4: Framing and narrative — how the choice of what to cover shapes what people believe
  • Day 5: Identity and community — how media environments construct group membership and belonging

Week 2 — Power

  • Day 6: Platform incentives and governance — the economic logic behind the design choices you live with
  • Day 7: Policy and speech — the legal and regulatory landscape governing content moderation
  • Day 8: Measurement and methods — how researchers study media effects and what those methods miss
  • Day 9: Case study 1 — the news ecosystem, local media collapse, and the political consequences
  • Day 10: Case study 2 — creator economies, brand integration, and the blurring of editorial and commercial

Week 3 — Harms, Benefits, and Action

  • Day 11: Harms — misinformation, coordinated harassment, algorithmic amplification of extremism
  • Day 12: Benefits — access, education, community formation, and the mobilizations that worked
  • Day 13: Interventions — what product changes, platform policies, and regulatory approaches have actually worked
  • Day 14: Briefing practice — compress your analysis into something actionable for a decision-maker
  • Day 15: Final presentation and memo — your media analysis, presented and defended

What You’ll Make

  • Two media briefs analyzing specific platforms, cases, or trends with evidence and clear framing
  • A case presentation on a media system or event, with stakeholder analysis and policy implications
  • A final synthesis memo connecting the course frameworks to a real, current media challenge

Key Practices

  • Separate description, analysis, and judgment — these are three different activities and conflating them weakens all three
  • Cite sources and document your analytical framework — media analysis without methodological transparency is just opinion
  • Write for clarity and decision-relevance — the goal is to make someone better at their job, not to demonstrate your fluency with theory
