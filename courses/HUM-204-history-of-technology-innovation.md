# HUM-204 — History of Technology & Innovation

Every technology that disrupts the world today has already disrupted it before — the patterns are there, if you know how to look.

## The Central Question

> Does technology change the world — or does the world decide what technology means?

## What This Course Is About

This course explores how transformative technologies — the printing press, steam power, electricity, the internet, semiconductors — actually changed the societies that adopted them. Not just what they enabled, but how they were resisted, shaped, repurposed, and eventually institutionalized. You'll learn to read technological change as a historical process driven by economics, politics, and culture — not just invention — and you'll use that understanding to make sharper judgments about the technologies reshaping the world right now.

## Why It Matters Now

The people building AI and other transformative technologies are making consequential decisions without much historical perspective. History doesn't repeat, but it rhymes — and the patterns of diffusion, regulation, concentration, and unintended consequence that shaped prior technological waves are already visible in the current one.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Trace major technological transitions and analyze the social, economic, and political forces shaping them
- Apply frameworks for understanding diffusion, complementary inventions, and institutional adaptation
- Map the unintended consequences and second-order effects that emerged from past technological change
- Write case studies that extract usable insights without oversimplifying historical complexity
- Apply historical analogies to present-day technology choices with appropriate caveats

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Methods and Early Cases**

- Day 1: Why tech history? How to read the past without being nostalgic about it
- Day 2: Printing and the republic of letters — what happens when information becomes cheap
- Day 3: Steam, factories, and labor — the original disruption and what it did to workers and cities
- Day 4: Electricity and networks — how a general-purpose technology takes decades to change everything
- Day 5: Mass production and quality — Ford, Toyota, and the industrial systems that shaped the 20th century

**Week 2 — The Digital Transition**

- Day 6: Computing — from mainframes to personal computers and the democratization of processing
- Day 7: The internet and platforms — network effects, attention economies, and winner-take-all dynamics
- Day 8: Semiconductors — Moore's Law, supply chain concentration, and the geopolitics of chips
- Day 9: Energy transitions — the slow speed of infrastructure change and why it matters
- Day 10: War, research, and state capacity — DARPA, the Manhattan Project, and the public origins of private innovation

**Week 3 — Institutions and Implications**

- Day 11: Standards, IP, and regulation — how institutions shape what technologies become
- Day 12: Globalization and supply chains — the fragility built into efficiency
- Day 13: Case writing workshop — develop and draft your historical case study
- Day 14: Presentations — share your case and defend your analysis
- Day 15: Synthesis and lessons — what the arc from printing press to AI suggests about where we are

## What You'll Make

- Two historical case studies of 1,000–1,500 words, each extracting specific lessons with honest caveats
- A briefing on a current technology with historical analogs and what they do and don't predict
- A final presentation with recommendations for navigating a present-day technological transition

## Key Practices

- Seek primary sources and check the myths — most widely repeated tech history is simplified to the point of distortion
- Distinguish correlation from causation — technology doesn't cause social change by itself
- Make the lessons explicit: good historical analysis ends with specific, falsifiable claims about the present
