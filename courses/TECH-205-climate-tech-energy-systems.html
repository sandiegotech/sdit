TECH-205 — Climate Tech & Energy Systems

Climate change is a technical problem wrapped in a political problem wrapped in an economic problem — and you can’t solve any layer without understanding the others.

The Central Question

What will it take to power civilization without destroying it?

What This Course Is About

This course gives you a systems-level understanding of energy and climate: how civilization produces and uses energy, what carbon emissions actually come from, and what the real constraints on decarbonization are. You’ll work with actual numbers — building back-of-envelope models, stress-testing technology claims, and analyzing the grid and market constraints that determine what’s possible on what timeline. The goal is not optimism or pessimism, but honest quantitative thinking about one of the most complex problems humans have ever faced.

Why It Matters Now

Every company, government, and institution on earth is making consequential decisions about energy and climate. The people who understand these systems at a technical and economic level — who can tell you whether a given technology is at the right cost curve to matter at scale — are the ones shaping those decisions. This is a course that makes you useful in that conversation.

By the End of This Course, You Will

  • Model basic energy supply, demand, and emissions scenarios using real data
  • Compare energy technologies honestly — on cost, scale, timing, and system integration
  • Analyze grid constraints, storage requirements, and the economics of the energy transition
  • Evaluate policy instruments and market mechanisms for climate impact
  • Write a decision brief presenting a technology or policy option with full assumptions, scenarios, and sensitivities

The 15-Day Arc

Week 1 — The Numbers

  • Day 1: Energy units and orders of magnitude — the arithmetic that separates real solutions from wishful thinking
  • Day 2: Carbon accounting — where emissions actually come from and what net zero actually means
  • Day 3: The electricity grid — load, capacity, dispatch, and why renewables change everything
  • Day 4: Generation technologies — fossil, nuclear, solar, wind: the cost curves that matter
  • Day 5: Storage — batteries, pumped hydro, thermal, hydrogen, and the grid integration problem

Week 2 — The Hard Problems

  • Day 6: Efficiency and electrification — the overlooked half of the transition
  • Day 7: Industry and heat — the emissions that electricity can’t easily reach
  • Day 8: Transport and fuels — EVs, shipping, aviation, and the last-mile problem
  • Day 9: Carbon removal and sequestration — what it is, what it isn’t, and when we need it
  • Day 10: Policy and markets — carbon pricing, subsidies, standards, and what actually moves investment

Week 3 — Putting It Together

  • Day 11: Land, permitting, and social license — the non-technical constraints that stop projects
  • Day 12: Supply chains and critical materials — lithium, copper, rare earths, and geopolitical exposure
  • Day 13: Scenario modeling — building and stress-testing a decarbonization scenario
  • Day 14: Brief writing — visualizing results and communicating uncertainty honestly
  • Day 15: Final presentation — your technology or policy analysis, with model workbook

What You’ll Make

  • Three problem sets covering dimensional analysis, carbon accounting, and technology comparison
  • A technology brief analyzing one energy technology with data, assumptions, and an honest assessment of limits
  • A final presentation with a scenario model showing pathways, trade-offs, and your recommendations

Key Practices

  • Do the dimensional analysis first — if the numbers don’t work at scale, the technology doesn’t either
  • Show all assumptions explicitly and run sensitivities on the ones that matter most
  • Compare solutions by cost per ton, scale potential, and time to deployment — not just theoretical potential
