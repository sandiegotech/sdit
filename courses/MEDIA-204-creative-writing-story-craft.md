# MEDIA-204 — Creative Writing & Story Craft

Writing is one of the few things that gets harder the more you know — but only because you start to see the gap between what you meant and what you said.

## The Central Question

> What is the difference between a story that is told and one that is felt?

## What This Course Is About

This is a writing workshop course. You'll write short pieces every week — across narrative, essay, and dialogue — and submit them for peer critique. The craft concepts you'll study (structure, voice, revision, scene, and economy) are introduced not as rules but as tools for solving specific problems in your own work. The method is simple: produce, share, receive feedback, revise. The goal is a regular, sustainable writing practice and a body of finished work that demonstrates real development.

## Why It Matters Now

AI can produce grammatically correct, structurally sound writing on almost any topic. What it cannot do is write from a specific, embodied human perspective — one that has lived something, noticed something, and found the words that make a reader feel the same. That distinctly human capacity is what this course develops.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Draft short works in multiple forms — narrative, essay, dialogue, scene — with control and intention
- Develop a distinctive voice and use it consistently across different formats and subjects
- Edit ruthlessly for clarity, rhythm, and economy without losing the life of the prose
- Participate in and facilitate workshop discussions with generosity and rigor
- Build a writing practice that continues beyond the course

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Foundation**

- Day 1: Habits and practice — what a writing routine actually looks like and why it matters
- Day 2: Observation and notes — training yourself to notice what other people overlook
- Day 3: Structure — beginnings that earn the reader's trust, middles that hold it, endings that matter
- Day 4: Voice and point of view — the decisions that make writing feel inhabited
- Day 5: Rhetoric — ethos, pathos, logos, and how nonfiction writing earns its claims

**Week 2 — Craft**

- Day 6: Dialogue and scene — how to put people in a room and let the reader be there
- Day 7: Description and imagery — specificity as the ground of all good writing
- Day 8: Argument and evidence — structuring a case that a skeptical reader will follow
- Day 9: Revision as discovery — how the second draft finds what the first draft was really trying to say
- Day 10: Style — economy, rhythm, and the sentence-level decisions that accumulate into a voice

**Week 3 — Workshop**

- Day 11: Forms and their demands — the essay, the story, the profile, and what each requires
- Day 12: Workshop 1 — your first piece, read and discussed by the group
- Day 13: Workshop 2 — a second piece, revised in light of the first workshop's lessons
- Day 14: Edit and polish — deep revision on your best piece with specific goals
- Day 15: Reading and reflection — share what you've made, trace your development, plan what's next

## What You'll Make

- Weekly short pieces of 500–800 words across different forms, with revision notes
- One longer revised piece of 1,200–2,000 words, submitted with draft history showing your revision process
- A craft notebook documenting the tools you've developed and the writing you've analyzed

## Key Practices

- Write every day, even badly — the practice is the thing, not the output of any single session
- Read aloud before submitting — your ear catches what your eye misses
- Take workshop feedback in writing and sit with it before responding — defensiveness is expensive
