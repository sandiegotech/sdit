# TECH-204 — Hardware Hacking & Embedded Systems

Software runs on hardware, and when you understand the hardware — how it's actually wired, powered, and programmed — everything about software gets clearer.

## The Central Question

> What happens when you understand machines from the inside out?

## What This Course Is About

This is a hands-on embedded systems course. You'll start with a microcontroller and a blinking LED and work up to sensors, actuators, communication protocols, and a small finished project you designed yourself. Every session involves building, measuring, and debugging something physical — and documenting it with enough precision that you (or someone else) could reproduce it. The emphasis is on understanding what's actually happening in the hardware, not just following tutorials until something works.

## Why It Matters Now

The world runs on embedded systems: every sensor, motor controller, IoT device, drone, and medical instrument is a small computer running firmware someone wrote. As software increasingly controls physical systems, the engineers who can bridge software and hardware — who can pick up a multimeter, read a datasheet, and debug a circuit — are the ones who build the things that matter.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Read schematics and build circuits on a breadboard safely and confidently
- Configure and program a microcontroller to interface with sensors and actuators
- Use serial communication protocols (UART, I2C, SPI) to connect peripheral devices
- Debug hardware and firmware systematically — and document your process
- Deliver a finished embedded project with full documentation, demo, and write-up

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Fundamentals**

- Day 1: Safety, tools, and the first program — blink an LED and understand why it works
- Day 2: Digital I/O and debounce — reading buttons, controlling outputs
- Day 3: Timers and PWM — controlling speed and brightness precisely
- Day 4: UART and logging — getting data off your device and onto your screen
- Day 5: I2C and SPI — connecting sensors and reading real-world data

**Week 2 — Expanding Capability**

- Day 6: ADC and signal basics — reading analog sensors, understanding noise
- Day 7: Interrupts and low-power modes — responsive systems that don't waste energy
- Day 8: Motor and actuator control — making things move
- Day 9: State machines and robustness — writing firmware that handles the unexpected
- Day 10: From breadboard to PCB — introduction to board design and layout

**Week 3 — Building and Shipping**

- Day 11: Packaging and enclosures — your project needs to live somewhere
- Day 12: Testing and fault injection — deliberately breaking things to find weaknesses
- Day 13: Mini-project build — your design, your problem, your solution
- Day 14: Debug and polish — the last 20% that takes 80% of the time
- Day 15: Demo day — present your project, explain your choices, reflect on what you'd do differently

## What You'll Make

- A lab portfolio: four documented lab exercises from blink through sensor integration to actuator control
- A final embedded project of your own design, with schematic, firmware, demo video, and written explanation
- A debugging log from at least two sessions, showing how you diagnosed and fixed real problems

## Key Practices

- Measure before you power — a multimeter check takes ten seconds and saves hours
- Version your firmware and document every hardware change; ambiguity is how bugs hide
- Start with the simplest possible thing that works, then add complexity one step at a time
