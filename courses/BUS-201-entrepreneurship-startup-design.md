# BUS-201 — Entrepreneurship & Startup Design

Most ideas don't fail because they're bad — they fail because the people behind them never found out what it would actually take to make them real.

## The Central Question

> What is the difference between a good idea and a real venture?

## What This Course Is About

This course is a simulation of the early-stage entrepreneurial process — accelerated, structured, and deliberately uncomfortable. You'll identify a real problem, talk to real people who have it, build a rough solution, test it, and figure out whether there's a viable business underneath. Every week produces something tangible: a canvas, a prototype, a pitch, a financial model. The emphasis is on moving fast and learning from what you find out, not on having a brilliant idea at the start.

## Why It Matters Now

The tools for starting something — no-code software, AI-assisted prototyping, global distribution platforms — have never been more accessible. What's scarce now is the judgment to tell a real opportunity from a self-flattering assumption, and the discipline to test the difference quickly. That's what this course builds.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Conduct genuine customer discovery interviews and extract actionable insights
- Build and test a minimum viable product using lean methods
- Develop a credible financial model including revenue, costs, and funding needs
- Construct a Lean Canvas that maps your riskiest assumptions and tests them
- Present a compelling, evidence-based pitch to a skeptical audience

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Problem and Customer**

- Day 1: Team formation and problem space selection — find a pain, not a product
- Day 2: Opportunity recognition — market research, trend analysis, and unmet needs
- Day 3: Customer discovery — how to run an interview that actually tells you something
- Day 4: Ideation and value proposition — from insights to possible solutions
- Day 5: Lean Canvas v1 — map your riskiest hypotheses before you build anything

**Week 2 — Build and Test**

- Day 6: Prototyping basics — low-fidelity first, always
- Day 7: MVP testing — get your prototype in front of real people and watch what happens
- Day 8: Business models — revenue streams, cost structures, and the unit economics that matter
- Day 9: Financial literacy — burn rate, runway, and the simple model that keeps you honest
- Day 10: Legal and ethics basics — IP, incorporation, and the obligations you're taking on

**Week 3 — Scale and Communicate**

- Day 11: Team and culture — how founders divide equity, roles, and decisions
- Day 12: Go-to-market strategy — your first customers and how you reach them
- Day 13: Pitch craft — the structure, the story, and the slide that matters most
- Day 14: Practice pitches — present to peers, get hard feedback, revise
- Day 15: Demo day — final pitch, executive summary, and honest reflection on what you'd do next

## What You'll Make

- A Lean Canvas with documented customer discovery evidence behind every hypothesis
- A prototype and user testing report showing what you learned and what you changed
- A pitch deck and live presentation, plus a written executive summary of the venture

## Key Practices

- Talk to ten real people before building anything — the market is smarter than your assumptions
- Document every customer interview and every major pivot — the learning is the asset
- Your financial model should be simple enough to explain in two minutes and honest enough to survive a hard question
