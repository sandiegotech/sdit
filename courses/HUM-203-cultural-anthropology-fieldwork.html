HUM-203 — Cultural Anthropology & Fieldwork

The hardest part of understanding another culture isn’t learning about it — it’s learning to see your own assumptions as assumptions.

The Central Question

What does it mean to understand a culture that isn’t your own — without reducing it to yours?

What This Course Is About

This course teaches the methods of cultural anthropology through practice. You’ll learn ethnographic observation, semi-structured interviewing, and field note-taking — and you’ll apply them to real communities, including your own. The emphasis is on ethics, precision, and epistemic humility: keeping your observations separate from your interpretations, obtaining genuine consent, and representing people’s lives in ways they would recognize as fair. You’ll conduct a small original fieldwork project and write it up as an ethnographic report.

Why It Matters Now

User research, product design, community organizing, and international operations all require the ability to understand how people actually live — not how you assume they live. The ethnographic habit of mind, developed in this course, is directly applicable to any work that involves crossing cultural or social boundaries.

By the End of This Course, You Will

  • Conduct participant observation and semi-structured interviews using proper ethical protocols
  • Maintain rigorous field notes that separate observation from interpretation
  • Analyze patterns across your data using coding and thematic analysis
  • Write an ethnographic report that describes a community’s practices honestly and respectfully
  • Reflect on your own cultural assumptions and how they shape what you notice

The 15-Day Arc

Week 1 — Methods

  • Day 1: What is culture? The concept, its uses, and its limits
  • Day 2: Observation and participation — how to watch without collapsing the distance
  • Day 3: Interviewing — consent, question design, listening, and what you do with what you hear
  • Day 4: Field notes — structure, discipline, and the habit of recording before interpreting
  • Day 5: Ethics and power — representation, consent, and the responsibilities of the researcher

Week 2 — In the Field

  • Day 6: Site selection and access — how to enter a community without distorting it
  • Day 7: Pilot study and debrief — your first real field session, reflected on honestly
  • Day 8: Coding and theming — finding patterns across your notes without imposing them
  • Day 9: Artifacts and material culture — what people own and use and make tells you things interviews don’t
  • Day 10: Comparative frames — how to situate your findings without exoticizing them

Week 3 — Writing and Applying

  • Day 11: Visual and audio methods — photography, recording, and their ethical complications
  • Day 12: From notes to narrative — the craft of ethnographic writing
  • Day 13: Peer review and revision — reading each other’s drafts for gaps and assumptions
  • Day 14: Policy and design implications — what your fieldwork findings suggest for people who build things
  • Day 15: Final report and reflection — your ethnographic study, presented and discussed

What You’ll Make

  • A fieldwork portfolio including observation notes, interview notes, and coding memos from at least three sessions
  • A cultural map and practice inventory for your chosen site
  • A final ethnographic report of 2,000–3,000 words with evidence, analysis, and honest acknowledgment of limitations

Key Practices

  • Separate what you saw from what you think it means — this is harder than it sounds and more important than it seems
  • Return to your participants — check your interpretations against the people whose lives you’re describing
  • Practice active listening in interviews; the best data comes from following the thread, not the script
