BUS-202 — Finance for Innovators

Most founders run out of money not because they spent too much, but because they didn’t understand what the numbers were telling them until it was too late.

The Central Question

How does money move — and how do you make it work for your mission?

What This Course Is About

This course teaches the financial fundamentals every founder and builder needs: how to read the three core financial statements, how to model unit economics and runway, and how to choose a funding path that matches your stage and strategy. You’ll work with real spreadsheets, real frameworks, and real scenarios — building a financial model for a venture of your own and stress-testing it against hard questions. No accounting background required; just a willingness to deal honestly with numbers.

Why It Matters Now

Access to capital is no longer the bottleneck it once was for early-stage ventures. Financial literacy is. The founders who make good decisions about burn, pricing, and fundraising are the ones who survive long enough to figure out what they’re actually building.

By the End of This Course, You Will

  • Read and interpret an income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement
  • Model unit economics including CAC, LTV, contribution margin, and payback period
  • Build a runway model with scenarios and sensitivity analysis
  • Understand funding options — grants, revenue-based, equity — and the trade-offs of each
  • Write a concise financing memo that gives decision-makers a clear recommendation

The 15-Day Arc

Week 1 — The Statements

  • Day 1: Accounting basics — the concepts and vocabulary that underpin everything else
  • Day 2: The income statement — revenue, COGS, gross margin, and what the P&L is actually telling you
  • Day 3: Cash flow — the difference between profit and cash, and why it matters
  • Day 4: The balance sheet — assets, liabilities, equity, and how the three statements connect
  • Day 5: Unit economics and contribution margin — the math that tells you if the business works

Week 2 — Models and Decisions

  • Day 6: CAC and LTV — customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, and cohort analysis
  • Day 7: Pricing and revenue models — subscriptions, transactions, usage, and how they affect everything
  • Day 8: Cost modeling — fixed vs. variable, headcount planning, and the burn rate that matters
  • Day 9: Scenario planning — sensitivity analysis, what-ifs, and the assumptions you should stress-test
  • Day 10: Funding paths — non-dilutive vs. dilutive options and when each makes sense

Week 3 — Capitalization and Communication

  • Day 11: Cap tables — equity, options, dilution, and what you’re giving up
  • Day 12: Investor readiness — the metrics that matter and how to present them clearly
  • Day 13: Working session — build your model with real numbers and real assumptions
  • Day 14: Peer review and revisions — stress-test each other’s models
  • Day 15: Final memo and model walkthrough — present your financial analysis and defend your assumptions

What You’ll Make

  • A complete financial model with income statement, cash flow projection, and runway analysis
  • A scenario analysis showing at least three funding paths with trade-offs clearly labeled
  • A financing memo of two to three pages recommending a funding strategy with justification

Key Practices

  • Label every assumption in your model — if you can’t explain where a number comes from, it doesn’t belong
  • Run at least a pessimistic, base, and optimistic scenario — false precision is worse than honest uncertainty
  • Tie spending decisions to learning milestones, not growth metrics
