LBS-101 — The Mental Gym

Philosophy, logic, and the practice of thinking clearly — because the quality of your life depends on the quality of your thinking.

The Central Question

What does it mean to think clearly — and why does it matter more now than ever before?

What This Course Is About

This course trains the mind. You’ll read philosophy, practice formal and informal logic, study how arguments are structured, and examine the assumptions you didn’t know you were making. The goal is not to memorize positions — it’s to become someone who thinks better.

Why It Matters Now

AI can generate convincing text, plausible arguments, and sophisticated-sounding analysis. The skill it cannot replicate is the judgment to evaluate whether those arguments are actually sound. That judgment is what this course builds.

By the End of This Course, You Will

  • Identify and dismantle flawed arguments, including your own
  • Apply classical and contemporary philosophical frameworks to real questions
  • Write a defensible personal philosophy of knowledge and education
  • Recognize how assumptions, bias, and framing shape what feels like “obvious truth”

The 15-Day Arc

Week 1 — The Examined Life

  • Day 1: What is a liberal education? The question behind the question
  • Day 2: Socrates, the examined life, and the courage to not know
  • Day 3: What is logic? Arguments, validity, and soundness
  • Day 4: Fallacies — the twenty most dangerous patterns of bad thinking
  • Day 5: Truth, belief, and the difference between them

Week 2 — How We Know

  • Day 6: Epistemology — what knowledge actually requires
  • Day 7: Rhetoric and persuasion — how to move people, and when you shouldn’t
  • Day 8: Writing as thinking — clarity is the test of understanding
  • Day 9: Bias, perception, and the limits of your own point of view
  • Day 10: Worldviews — the invisible frameworks we all think inside

Week 3 — Philosophy in Practice

  • Day 11: Ethics and moral reasoning — how to think about what to do
  • Day 12: The limits of knowledge — what we cannot know and why that matters
  • Day 13: Technology, AI, and the tools of thought
  • Day 14: AI as a mirror — what chatbots reveal about how humans think
  • Day 15: My philosophy of learning — synthesis and personal statement

What You’ll Make

  • A logic journal documenting 10 arguments encountered in the real world (valid, invalid, fallacious)
  • A 2-page epistemological position paper: what you believe knowledge requires
  • A final 1-page philosophy of learning, written for yourself

Key Practices

  • Argue both sides of every question before choosing one
  • Write before you read — put your starting position on paper so you can see it change
  • Find one example of a fallacy or bad argument every day in the wild
