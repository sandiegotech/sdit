# LBS-220 — Scientific Method & Experimental Design

Most bad science isn't dishonest — it's just poorly designed, and this course teaches you the difference between a study that can actually tell you something and one that can't.

## The Central Question

> How do you know what you know — and how strong is the evidence?

## What This Course Is About

You'll learn to design experiments that can actually answer questions: framing hypotheses, controlling variables, measuring with appropriate precision, and handling uncertainty honestly. You'll run a small experiment of your own and document it in enough detail that someone else could reproduce it. The emphasis throughout is on epistemic honesty — knowing what your data supports and what it doesn't, and saying so clearly.

## Why It Matters Now

AI tools can now generate plausible-sounding scientific claims at scale. The ability to evaluate experimental design — to ask whether a study was actually controlled, whether the sample was representative, whether the effect size matters — is one of the most urgent critical thinking skills of this decade.

## By the End of This Course, You Will

- Frame testable hypotheses and identify confounding variables
- Design controlled studies with appropriate sample sizes and measurement strategies
- Collect, visualize, and reason about data with uncertainty in mind
- Evaluate published studies for design flaws, statistical claims, and reproducibility
- Document methods clearly enough that another student could replicate your work

## The 15-Day Arc

**Week 1 — Thinking Scientifically**

- Day 1: What scientific reasoning actually is — and what it isn't
- Day 2: Hypotheses, variables, and the anatomy of a testable claim
- Day 3: Measurement — units, precision, accuracy, and error
- Day 4: Experimental design — controls, randomization, and blinding
- Day 5: Sampling and bias — who's in your study, and why it matters

**Week 2 — Doing the Work**

- Day 6: Data collection — protocols, instruments, and record-keeping
- Day 7: Visualization fundamentals — histograms, scatter plots, error bars
- Day 8: Statistical thinking — distributions, means, variance, and what p-values actually mean
- Day 9: Reproducibility and open science — pre-registration, data sharing, and replication
- Day 10: Your experiment — design review and ethics check

**Week 3 — Communicating Science**

- Day 11: Field methods — observation protocols and environmental measurement
- Day 12: Reading papers critically — spotting design flaws and overstated conclusions
- Day 13: Peer review — how it works and why it sometimes doesn't
- Day 14: Case studies in scientific failure and recovery
- Day 15: Poster presentation — your experiment, your results, your honest assessment

## What You'll Make

- A pre-registered experimental design document for your own study
- A lab notebook with raw data, analysis, and honest reporting of what worked and what didn't
- A poster or short paper presenting your experiment in the format of a scientific communication

## Key Practices

- Pre-register your hypothesis before collecting data — decide what would count as a result before you see one
- Keep a lab notebook in real time; memory is not a reliable instrument
- Report null results and failed attempts — they are data too
