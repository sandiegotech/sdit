Day 04 — What Do You Actually See?

Course: LBS 120 – Physics with Lab

Session Focus

There is a difference between seeing something and explaining it.

Most of the time, we skip directly to the explanation. We see a bird and think “bird.” We see a shadow moving and think “the sun.” We stop looking the moment we have a name for what we’re looking at.

Physics starts by refusing to do that. It asks you to slow down and describe exactly what is happening — before you explain it, before you name it, before you fit it into a category. Raw observation, nothing added.

This is harder than it sounds. And it’s one of the most important things you’ll learn to do.

“I was born not knowing and have had only a little time to change that here and there.” — Richard Feynman

What You’ll Do Today

Time: ~60 minutes Output: A 10-minute observation log — pure description, no explanation

Session Flow

1. Begin with this question (5 min)

Write your immediate answer:

When was the last time you looked at something without immediately trying to explain or categorize it? What did you actually see?

If you can’t remember a specific moment, write about what it would feel like to stop explaining and just look.

2. Read (15 min)

Richard Feynman — “The Pleasure of Finding Things Out” (excerpt) Read at MIT Media Lab

Read the first 10–12 pages. Feynman describes what it feels like to be genuinely curious about how things work — not to pass a test, but because the world is strange and interesting and worth looking at directly.

Pay attention to the story about the flower. That story is the whole session.

3. Watch (10 min)

Veritasium — “Is Most Published Research Wrong?” Watch on YouTube

This isn’t about the research replication crisis specifically. Watch it as a lesson about what happens when we trust our explanations more than our observations — when we start with the answer and work backward to fit the evidence.

4. Your observation session (10 min)

Find something that is physically moving or changing. Outside is best, but a window works. A candle flame, running water, or a fan also work.

Set a timer for 10 minutes.

Watch. Write what you see. Follow these rules:

Allowed: “The shadow moved left.” “The water hit the edge and split into two streams.” “The flame bent right, then came back.”

Not allowed: “Because of the wind…” “Due to gravity…” “This happens because…”

No explanations. No because. No therefore. Only: what happened, in what order, at what speed.

Practice Block

Your Observation Log

Write everything you observed during your 10 minutes. Full sentences. Present tense or past tense — your choice. Just what happened.

Then answer these three questions:

1. What surprised you? — Something you noticed that you wouldn’t have expected.

2. What question did it raise? — The thing you found yourself wanting to explain but forced yourself not to.

3. Where did you catch yourself explaining instead of observing? — Be honest. It’s nearly impossible to observe purely. Notice when you slipped.

AI Lens (Optional)

Take your observation log — just the raw observations, no questions — and share it with your AI assistant. Ask:

“Based on this description, what is physically happening here? What are the underlying principles?”

Then read the explanation. Compare it to the raw thing you watched.

Ask yourself: does the explanation make the observation more or less interesting? Does naming the physics change your relationship to the thing you watched?

Reflection to Carry Forward

Feynman says his father taught him to look at things and ask “what’s actually happening here?” rather than just naming them. Who taught you to look? Who taught you to explain instead of look?

One paragraph.

Hard Problem (Optional)

The Feynman Technique

Pick the phenomenon you observed today. Now explain it — actually explain it, from scratch, in plain language, as if to someone who has never studied physics.

Where do you get stuck? What do you actually understand versus what have you just memorized the name for?

Write down the exact moment where your explanation runs out. That boundary — the edge of your actual understanding — is the most valuable thing you found today.

Notes

  • The hardest part of this session is the “no explanations” rule during observation. Most people last about 30 seconds before slipping into interpretation. That’s normal. Just notice when you do it.
  • Outside is better than inside. Motion is more interesting than static objects.
  • Your observation log doesn’t have to be beautiful prose. Short, precise sentences are better than long, flowery ones.
