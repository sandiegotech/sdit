The Nine Laws of Course Design

Use science to make your art better. Use art to make your science better.

  1. The Founder's TestWould the founder genuinely want to take this course? If not, redesign or cut.
  2. The Dual RegisterKeep the names, transform the delivery. The catalog says Calculus I; the classroom is the ocean, the ramp, the studio.
  3. Physical Before FormalNo concept arrives as notation first. Force is a bottom turn before it is a vector.
  4. The Double FeatureIdeas travel in company — a text, a film, an album, discussed as one conversation. Media embedded, never linked away.
  5. It Ends in a Made ThingEvery course closes with an artifact, defended or exhibited. Understanding is only completed in the making.
  6. A Day Looks EasyOne idea, one thing to experience, one question — done in minutes. Depth waits behind optional doors. Overwhelm is a design failure.
  7. AI-Native, Not AI-DependentThe tools stay open in every course — and the human is regularly examined unaided.
  8. Open to EveryoneEvery course published in full, free, to anyone on Earth, without asking permission — and readable without an account. Non-negotiable and permanent.
  9. Day by Day, or It Doesn't ShipA syllabus is a promise; the daily path is the course. Nothing is called live until a learner knows exactly what to do each day.

Every course serves the five outcomes —
Judgment · Command of Intelligence · Craft · Articulation · Breadth of Mind
