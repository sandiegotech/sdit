The Degree

Bachelor of Arts in Technology — Working Framework San Diego Institute of Technology · Companion to The Plan · June 2026

1. Degree Overview

One degree — 120 semester units, 8 semesters. Every student earns the Bachelor of Arts in Technology. The first two years are the Foundation, identical for everyone: a common language and framework — the books, the mathematics, the code, the Workshop. At the end of Year 2 each student sharpens their focus by declaring a concentration, chosen from experience — what they are interested in and good at, demonstrated across two years of made work:

  • Media — things people watch, hear, and feel: film, music production, interactive story, generative media.
  • Design — things people use: interfaces, interaction, fabrication, physical computing — where made things meet people.
  • Engineering — things that work on their own: AI systems, embedded sensors, robotics.

Structural philosophy: identical baseline for every student in Years 1–2 (zero electives — the shared formation is the product). Nothing is declared at the door, because at the door nobody truly knows; the Foundation is designed to show you. Then four depth studios in the declared concentration in Years 3–4 — plus the Crossing: one full depth studio from outside the concentration, required of everyone. The artist engineers; the engineer makes art. The depth catalog is eight studios total; the three concentration lists overlap by design, with Design sharing studios with both neighbors.

One degree is also the honest accreditation unit: BPPE and WSCUC review a single program — the Bachelor of Arts in Technology — and the concentrations live inside it.

Every course carries two registers: a catalog name (accreditation- and transfer-legible) and a teaching frame (how it is actually delivered). Both are listed below and in the catalog.

The standing test for every course: would the founder genuinely want to take it? If not, redesign or cut.

The Workshop (WKSP 101–402, 2 units/semester)

Every semester, every student, on a project team that ships. Materials rotate through the region’s craft traditions: surfboard and skateboard construction, amplifier and pedal electronics, motorcycle mechanics, camera rigs and housings, drones and ocean sensors. Years 2+ take external clients. Assessed by portfolio review and public exhibition.

4. The Concentrations & the Crossing (Years 3–4)

The depth catalog is eight 4-unit studios; each concentration prescribes four of them, and the lists overlap by design — Design shares studios with both Media and Engineering, because design is where made things meet people. Every student also takes one studio from outside their concentration — the Crossing. This requirement is the founding thesis made structural: exploring the other field is not extra credit.

5. Field Term & Capstone

FLD 301 — Field Term (4 units, Year 3). One full term embedded in a partner organization — engineering firm, defense tech company, lab, studio, label, or venture — under a designated mentor, with faculty advising. Assessed by mentor evaluation + portfolio + written field report.

CAP 401–402 — Thesis / Venture Capstone (4 units, Year 4). A defended original work in one of three accepted forms: (1) research thesis; (2) an engineered system that works; (3) a venture that exists — incorporated, with users or customers. Media capstones lean toward works that move people (a film, a record, an interactive piece); Design capstones toward products in real hands; Engineering capstones toward systems that run — but all three forms are open to every concentration. Public defense before faculty and outside examiners.

Teaching requirement (Year 4, non-credit, graduation-required): every fourth-year leads first-years in Workshop, Canon section, or Physical Culture. You don’t know it until you can teach it.

6. Open Studio Elective (4 units, Year 4)

The elective system is deliberately small and deliberately late — the shared core is the institution’s identity, and choice is earned after formation. One course, chosen from a rotating slate of practitioner-taught courses rather than a fixed departmental list. The slate changes every year; this is how the curriculum absorbs the new without rewriting the core.

Categories of the slate:

  • Visiting Masters — short courses taught by working practitioners: a shaper on advanced board design, a cinematographer on light, a founder on company formation, a forecaster on ocean modeling.
  • Language & Abroad — language study tied to the Physical Culture Foreign Term or a travel term.
  • Deep Cuts — faculty passion courses: cryptography, bookbinding, celestial navigation, the history of the synthesizer.
  • Cross-Concentration — a second depth studio from outside your concentration, beyond the required Crossing.

Elective courses still carry catalog-grade outcomes and assessment — the dual-register rule applies everywhere. Perspectives lessons (The Plan, §IV) frequently pilot here: a Perspective that resonates becomes an Open Studio course; an Open Studio course that proves core-worthy gets promoted into a thread of the catalog.

8. Gates & Assessment

  • Annual Gates (end of each year): portfolio review + written and oral examination against the five outcomes (Judgment, Command of Intelligence, Craft, Articulation, Breadth of Mind).
  • AI-native, not AI-dependent: AI tools are open and studied everywhere, and students pass periodic unassisted examinations in writing, mathematics, and code. The institution certifies the human, so it must regularly observe the human unaided.
  • Grades exist (transcripts require them); the portfolio is the primary artifact.
  • Every course description at catalog grade states learning outcomes mapped to the five institutional outcomes — this mapping is the WSCUC Standard 2 evidence file; an internal alignment standard enforces it course by course.

9. Scale Commitments

Cohorts of 30–50 in the founding years. Seminar cap 15. Faculty hired as practitioner-scholars with a documented “equivalent professional standing” rubric for non-terminal-degree masters. Small is the pedagogy and the only honest staffing plan — and it is why the whole catalog is roughly forty courses, all but the eight depth studios shared by everyone.

Working framework. To be developed to catalog grade (full course descriptions, outcome mappings, faculty qualifications) as the BPPE/WSCUC application core.
