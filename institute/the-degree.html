The Degree

Bachelor of Science in Technology — Working Framework San Diego Institute of Technology · Companion to The Plan · June 2026

1. Degree Overview

Bachelor of Science in Technology — 120 semester units, 8 semesters. One degree for all students; concentration declared at the end of Year 2:

  • Intelligent Systems — machine learning, autonomy, AI engineering and deployment
  • Applied Engineering — hardware, robotics, fabrication, embedded and physical systems
  • Computational Media & Design — interfaces, film, music technology, human-machine experience

Structural philosophy: identical baseline for every student in Years 1–2 (zero electives — the shared formation is the product), then specialization and choice in Years 3–4. Every course carries two registers: a catalog name (accreditation- and transfer-legible) and a teaching frame (how it is actually delivered). Both are listed below.

The standing test for every course: would the founder genuinely want to take it? If not, redesign or cut.

3. The Threads — Catalog Names & Teaching Frames

The Canon (HUM 101–402) — the Double Feature Method

Every unit pairs the text + a film + an album, discussed as one conversation. Each term ends with a made response — essay, short film, recorded piece, or designed object — defended in seminar.

The Workshop (WKSP 101–402, 2 units/semester)

Every semester, every student, on a project team that ships. Materials rotate through the region’s craft traditions: surfboard and skateboard construction, amplifier and pedal electronics, motorcycle mechanics, camera rigs and housings, drones and ocean sensors. Years 2+ take external clients. Assessed by portfolio review and public exhibition.

4. Concentrations (Years 3–4, 12 units + capstone alignment)

Each concentration is three advanced 4-unit courses plus a capstone in-track. Initial course sets:

  • Intelligent Systems: Autonomy & Robotics; Applied Deep Learning; AI Safety, Evaluation & Deployment.
  • Applied Engineering: Mechanical Design & Fabrication; Embedded Systems & Sensors; Energy & Ocean Systems.
  • Computational Media & Design: Interactive Systems & Interfaces; Film & Sound Production; Simulation & Generative Media.

5. Field Term & Capstone

FLD 301 — Field Term (4 units, Year 3). One full term embedded in a partner organization — engineering firm, defense tech company, lab, studio, or venture — under a designated mentor, with faculty advising. Assessed by mentor evaluation + portfolio + written field report.

CAP 401–402 — Thesis / Venture Capstone (4 units, Year 4). A defended original work in one of three accepted forms: (1) research thesis; (2) an engineered system that works; (3) a venture that exists — incorporated, with users or customers. Public defense before faculty and outside examiners.

Teaching requirement (Year 4, non-credit, graduation-required): every fourth-year leads first-years in Workshop, Canon section, or Physical Culture. You don’t know it until you can teach it.

6. Open Studio Electives (8 units, Years 3–4)

The elective system is deliberately small and deliberately late — the shared core is the institution’s identity, and choice is earned after formation. Eight units, chosen from a rotating slate of practitioner-taught courses rather than a fixed departmental list. The slate changes every year; this is how the curriculum absorbs the new without rewriting the core.

Categories of the slate:

  • Visiting Masters — short courses taught by working practitioners: a shaper on advanced board design, a cinematographer on light, a founder on company formation, a forecaster on ocean modeling.
  • Language & Abroad — language study tied to the Physical Culture Foreign Term or a travel term.
  • Deep Cuts — faculty passion courses: cryptography, bookbinding, celestial navigation, the history of the synthesizer.
  • Cross-Concentration — any concentration course outside the student’s own track.

Elective courses still carry catalog-grade outcomes and assessment — the dual-register rule applies everywhere. Perspectives lessons (The Plan, §IV) frequently pilot here: a Perspective that resonates becomes an Open Studio course; an Open Studio course that proves core-worthy gets promoted into a thread.

8. Gates & Assessment

  • Annual Gates (end of each year): portfolio review + written and oral examination against the five outcomes (Judgment, Command of Intelligence, Craft, Articulation, Breadth of Mind).
  • AI-native, not AI-dependent: AI tools are open and studied everywhere, and students pass periodic unassisted examinations in writing, mathematics, and code. The institution certifies the human, so it must regularly observe the human unaided.
  • Grades exist (transcripts require them); the portfolio is the primary artifact.
  • Every course description at catalog grade states learning outcomes mapped to the five institutional outcomes — this mapping is the WSCUC Standard 2 evidence file.

9. Scale Commitments

Cohorts of 30–50 in the founding years. Seminar cap 15. Faculty hired as practitioner-scholars with a documented “equivalent professional standing” rubric for non-terminal-degree masters. Small is the pedagogy and the only honest staffing plan.

Working framework. To be developed to catalog grade (full course descriptions, outcome mappings, faculty qualifications) as the BPPE/WSCUC application core.
